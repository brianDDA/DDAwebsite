New Jersey Auto Insurance Trends

Auto insurance pricing in New Jersey has been under sustained pressure from rising claim severity, higher repair and medical costs, and increased accident activity since the pandemic. These trends affect both personal auto and commercial auto policies.

Why this matters: Higher claims and losses directly impact premiums, underwriting, and minimum coverage recommendations for both individuals and businesses.

Premium Trends – Personal Auto

According to the National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC) , New Jersey’s combined average personal auto premium per insured vehicle increased from about $1,505 in 2019 to approximately $1,693 in 2023. Total written personal auto premium in the state grew from roughly $7.8 billion to more than $9.4 billion over the same period.

Accidents & Claim Severity

New Jersey State Police crash data shows that fatal crashes and fatalities increased sharply after 2020, peaking in 2021 and 2022 before improving slightly in 2023. Higher-speed accidents, distracted driving, and rising vehicle repair costs all contribute to more expensive claims.

Commercial impact: Fleet operators and contractors face the same driving environment, which is why commercial auto has been one of the hardest insurance lines to place and price.

Rising Auto Insurance Rates

Industry analyses summarized by NJ 101.5 / ValuePenguin show that New Jersey auto insurance premiums increased approximately 6% in 2023, followed by double-digit increases in 2024 and an additional 17.2% average increase in 2025. This places the expected average annual auto premium near $2,400.

New Jersey Liability Limits Increase in 2026

In addition to claim trends, New Jersey law is increasing the minimum required auto liability limits under P.L.2022, c.87.

  • Effective January 1, 2023: Minimum limits increased to $25,000 / $50,000 / $25,000 (bodily injury per person / per accident / property damage).
  • Effective January 1, 2026: Minimum bodily injury limits increase again to $35,000 per person / $70,000 per accident, with property damage remaining at $25,000.
Important: Higher required limits generally mean higher premiums. Many drivers and businesses already carry limits above the minimum, but those at or near minimum limits should expect noticeable pricing changes at renewal.

What This Means for NJ Drivers & Businesses

  • Premiums are rising due to both claim trends and legal requirements.
  • Carrying only minimum limits may expose you to serious out-of-pocket risk.
  • Commercial auto and fleet policies are especially sensitive to loss history.
  • Good driving records, safety programs, and proper limits matter more than ever.
